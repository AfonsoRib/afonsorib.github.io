Hyperacionalization

June 9, 2022

What is hyperacionality?

One of the characteristics that the enlightenment brought with it was the focus on rationality as a means of reaching truth. From this, a lot of wild and interesting ideas (at first sight) came to be born. Unlike the Greeks who use deductive reasoning, they approached the creation of knowledge using inductive reasoning, that is, through looking for patterns in nature and from that stipulate some rule that explains said phenomena. This isn’t necessarily bad per say; the problem lies with human nature. we tend to be more inclined to belive what it’s close to what we already believe so we, sometimes unknowably, try to prove our beliefs through “reasoning”. That’s how we can reach false conclusions while being “rational”. the logic may be correct, assuming the premises are true but in reality the premises we take for granted aren’t always true. I like to call this hyperacionality; When you use reason so much that you go beyond it. You are, in essence, building a house of cards that’s ready to fall. Let’s see an example of what I’m saying so that we can visualize it better!

The things we take for granted

Some sociological and economical works take for granted that man and woman are fully rational beings. That is, if confronted if two choices they will choose whats best for them. A good example of this would be Adam Smith’s concept of the Invisible Hand. If you have to do groceries what’s the most likely place where you will do it? The Invisible Hand would point you to the best place according to the cost/effectiveness of the product you want. The reality is that it doesn’t always work that way (see my post on markets). People may go to a super market or a small grocery shop instead of going to a regular market where the food is better and cheaper. And why is that? well, in this case I would say that it is out of habit maybe some laziness. Not really a logical reason. If we assume that the person is fully rational, it correctly follows that we would choose our best option. While observing the real world and not the world of our ideas we conclude that humans aren’t all that rational. The body is just a machine of vices if you don’t take a hold of it.

Using reason to prove our beliefs

Let’s say we have something that we believe in and, because of the ideas instilled on us by the enlightenment, don’t want it to be just an act of faith. Let’s take the example of communism. Marx didn’t find out about how and why communism was going to happen simply by studying economics and noticing some never noticed patterns in the graphs. He already had some set of beliefs and wanted to formalize it in paper so that others could see what he sees. This is all and good but he, like the previous example, takes for granted that humans are strictly rational beings. So, following the ideas mention in his books, which also were rational, they would end up in a communist utopia and that there was no other way. Reality is… It didn’t happen. “But dude, what happened in the soviet union was {insert excuse here}”. this is the most common criticism I see and it’s just shifting blame to something else which is funny because it also is racionalization that proves a persons belief. With this, I don’t intend to say that all that is arrived at through reason, even if the person already believed in that before, is false. There may be good material written in Marx’s books but some of his conclusions are plagued by hyperracionalism.

This has also happened to me. I’ve tried to justify my wrong doings with reason. Something on the line of “I can take a bit more of this because what they are paying is a steal!”. After noticing it I just decided to stop buying there or buy there but take what I am allowed.

Reason as a tool

With all this talk looks like I am making a bad picture out of reason. this is not the case. I just want more people to recognize that reason is not what it’s made out to be. Reason is a tool. A great one. But, just like a hammer. It can hurt pretty badly if we are not careful.
